What makes some languages more difficult to learn than others?¶

by Lauren Marar¶

This analysis will look at langauge difficulty for native English speakers and examine the linguistic features that distinguish easier and more difficult languages. Are there particular grammatical or phonological features that launch an otherwise ordinary language into the realm of difficult?

Note: Languages will be referred to as "easier" and "difficult" for short to represent their relative difficulty for an English speaker. Inherent difficulty independent of native language is not explored in this project.

This analysis will utilize two datasets:¶

  1. The World Atlas of Language Structures (WALS), which catalogues language features for 2,679 languages: https://www.kaggle.com/rtatman/world-atlas-of-language-structures
  1. Language difficulty ratings as determined by the Foreign Service for 67 frequently studied languages: https://www.state.gov/m/fsi/sls/c78549.htm

Langauge difficulty ratings¶

The Foreign Service categorized 67 languages into four difficulty levels. The difficulty levels were derived from the expected time it would take a native English speaker to reach "Professional Working Proficiency" (a score of 3 in speaking and reading on the Interagency Language Roundtable scale). English will be added as "level 0" for a comparison point.

Most languages within a family have the same difficulty rating. Other than the Indo-European family, only Austronesian, Sino-Tibetan, and Afro-Asiatic languages span more than one difficulty rating.

The Indo-European family has the largest range of difficulty levels, split primarily between category I and category III. This is intuitive as the category I genuses are Germanic and Romance (of Latin origin), which form the roots of English. The category III Indo-European languages comprise primarily the "Indo" part of the family, in other words, the South-Central Dravidian and Indic languages of South Asia.

But wait, there's more...

It looks like there is one Germanic language that is a category II difficulty and another that is a category III. Let's find out which ones they are:

German: Difficulty level 2 (36 weeks of study)

Icelandic: Difficulty level 3 (44 weeks of study)

It is interesting how both English and Icelandic are both Germanic languages, but are separated by 3 difficulty levels!

Conclusions¶

There are a handful of features that vary according to the difficulty of a language relative to English:

  • Whether the language uses prepositions or postpositions
  • How the language shows the relationship between participants of an event
  • Where the language places a relative clause with respect to the noun
  • Where the language places the interrogative phrase in a question
  • How the language aligns a verbal person marker
  • How the language expresses a negative indefinite pronoun
  • How the language forms a yes or no question
  • How the language describes ownership/posession
  • How the language encodes relative clauses

It is possible that some of these features might not actually make a language difficult (such as the absence of definite articles.) Moreover, there may be features not listed above that, when combined with several other features, raise the difficulty of a language. It may be that there are several ways to make a language difficult, by combining a number of unique features, such as that no single feature stands out as the leading cause of language difficulty.

Further research¶

  • A regression analysis may better identify if the interaction of multiple features contributes to a language's difficulty.
  • Another challenge was that the frequency of values associated with each linguistic feature were not evenly distributed. Binning values may help discern trends in difficulty.

Even further research¶

  • Because the sample size of very difficult languages was only 5, whereas the other difficulty groups were much larger, it may have made trends harder to spot as the frequency of occurrence in level 5 languages would jump from 40% to 60% with the addition of one language.
  • Furthermore, this analysis does not take into account the number of speakers. While it is difficult to come accross a clean dataset of population by language ID, endeavoring to collect this data would allow us to assess the commonality or rarity of linguistic features by number of speakers, accounting for the fact that languages vary widely by number of native speakers.

In conclusion, it may very well be that those rare category IV languages stem from the perfect storm of a unique set of features, that

Easy languages are all alike; every difficult language is difficult in its own way.
